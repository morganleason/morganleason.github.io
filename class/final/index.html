The Decline of Greek Life:
Nationally or at the University of Maryland

The University Maryland Fraternity row on May 3, 2023.

by Morgan S. Leason


Diversity in Greek Life:

At the University of Maryland, the Greek system appears to be on the decline. Greek Life makes up a sizeable percentage of the undergraduate population but is not very diverse. According to The Diamondback, 76% of Greek life are white students while only 4% are black students.


Academics in Greek Life:

There are benefits that come with the Greek Life community, such as a high GPA. Greek life holds up the UMD GPA average of 3.33, with an average member’s GPA of 3.44.

Not just at UMD, Greek life appears to have little to no effect on grades for females in the grander scheme. Males on the other hand showed a lower grade-point average within Greek Life.

The question remains: Do malpractices in Greek life reflect these lower grades in males?


Greek Life and Mal-behavior:

During the peak of Covid ‘19, Greek Life was especially problematic as several chapters contributed to outbreaks on college campuses. At the University of North Carolina, at least three COVID-19 clusters were linked to Greek houses, and members of one sorority were caught holding a party in violation of UNC’s Community Standards and North Carolina’s Phase 2 guidelines.

John Hechinger, the author of True Gentlemen: The Broken Pledge of America’s Fraternities, says, “Greek life has really, for generations, been a public health problem, and I think that’s coming to the fore in the era of COVID-19. At one point, an organization of insurers rated fraternities as just above toxic waste dumps.”

Greek life has also perpetuated racism among college students, with incidents such as the racist chant at the University of Oklahoma, in which two students were expelled, and the video of fraternity members mocking slavery at the University of Georgia, leading to the suspension of the greek chapter.

Sexual assault is another big problem in the Greek community. Studies have found that fraternity men are three times more likely to rape women than nonaffiliated classmates. Also, most gang rapes reported on campus are tied to fraternities; At USC, 29.7% of female undergraduates reported sexual penetration or touching that involved force or incapacitation by alcohol or drugs. Also, women in sororities are 74% more likely to experience rape than women unaffiliated with sororities, which could relate to their proximity to fraternity boys.

Vanderbilt created an Instagram account to share negative student experiences with the Greek system. Other colleges, like Northwestern University, Duke University, and the University of Richmond, created similar accounts, which has sparked a nationwide debate over Greek life’s place on college campuses.

Then there is the issue of hazing, which is responsible for the deaths of many college students. 2020 was the first year in 60 years without any hazing-related deaths in the U.S. hazing rituals, which often include binge drinking and mental and physical abuse to initiate new members into Greek life.


The Remaining 'Excuses':

So, why is nothing done about this? The answer is simple: Alumni.

Alumni of the Greek system are important to university fundraising and former members have power in many spheres. Greek alumni donate four times more than nonaffiliated peers to colleges.

Greek Life also has worked to improve diversity with the Agora fraternity, which roughly means “gathering place” in ancient Greek. The Agora will be a non-residential space exclusively for undergraduate members of the Multicultural Greek Council (MGC), the National Pan-Hellenic Council (NPHC) and their guests. The university has invested $1.2 million into the project, and the Student Government Association gave $97,400, which is part of $175,400 of surplus money allocated to support various student services and programs.

This year at UMD, about half as many potential new members went through the Panhellenic rush process, creating small pledge classes that are almost unable to support the fees for the organizations to remain on campus.

Could this be a trend at only the University of Maryland or a national phenomenon?
